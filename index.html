The future of cooking

Research and copywriting for inspirational thought calatogue

The way we cook, eat, buy and store our food is undergoing radical change.

Emerging technologies, from the Internet of Things to augmented reality, present us with endless possibilities to improve the way we produce and cook our food. But without good design, these will only ever be possibilities. To bring this technology into the home’s – and the hands – of everyday people, it has to be tailored into people-centred products and services.

And that’s where Designit comes in.

How might we introduce alternative foods such as insects into the Western diet – to eat and to grow?

The concept of eating insects isn’t new at all. On a global scale, these protein-packed bad boys have been an important part of the diet for millennia. Really, Western cultures are just late to adopt this food trend.

"It’s about a potential new Western culture of insect eating and breeding. … There is a great variety of food on our planet that we rarely consider."

'A Kit To Grow Bugs At Home, To Eat', FastCompany

But they’re not just good for you, they’re good for the environment. The amount of red meat we consume in the west is an ‘environmental nightmare’, and as population increases, it’s simply unsustainable. Eating insects isn’t so much a trend, as a necessity. The opportunity lies in making insects socially acceptable in the West, to eat and to grow.

Bringing data to life

‘Det Byggede Danmark’ transforms raw data into the fascinating stories of the built environment.

Hidden in plain sight

The Danes are famous for their high quality of life, and central to that standard of living is the built environment. It’s one of the biggest contributors to the Danish gross domestic product but, on an individual level, the built environment is so much more than that. It forms the homes, public spaces, roads and underground infrastructure that make up the framework of the nation. Furthermore, it contributes to the jobs, cultural heritage and even identity of more than 5.5 million Danes. It’s all too easy to take this for granted, so that’s why The Knowledge Centre for Housing Economics (BVC) wanted to get Danes thinking more about the vital role the built environment plays in their lives.

Numbers don’t always speak for themselves

The BVC has been collecting and analysing statistics on the built environment since 2008, but had challenges in communicating so much data to the public in an accessible and appealing way. Designit was tasked with curating this wealth of information into a series of magazines that would inform and inspire Danes. Together the team went through the BVC’s statistics, shaping the raw data into stories that could contextualise and humanise the numbers.

Engaging and comprehensive, ‘Det Byggede Danmark’ combines different perspectives – from personal interviews, to infographics and statistics, poetry, pictures, and technical articles – to put the spotlight on the hidden stories of the Danish built environment. Crucial to the magazines’ success is the distinct visual language created by the design team. Uniquely, the visuals provide more than just ‘decoration’, but play a central role in telling these stories. Traditional graphs are joined by innovative and striking visual representations of data, while even the smallest details play their part in communicating information, from typography to textures. With such a unique blend of visual storytelling and careful content curation, the team has succeeded in bringing the Danish built environment to life, and opening it up to a whole new audience.

‘Det Byggede Danmark’ was recently awarded an iF Design Award.

The changing landscape of service design

Forrester Research has released its ‘Vendor Landscape: Service Design Agency Overview, 2015’. The key trends? The service design industry has changed dramatically in just two years, and Designit is one of the agencies included in the report.

Service design is in flux

Including a survey fielded to 70 service design agencies, this year’s Forrester ‘Service Design Agency Overview’ reveals a dramatic shift in the industry. Just two years ago, smaller agencies dominated the industry from Europe. Today, a more varied profile is emerging. Through a series of agency mergers and acquisitions, the service design industry has grown in recent years. Now these larger firms are changing the service design game as full-service digital and business systems providers.

Five flavours of service design

So what exactly does it mean to be ‘full-service’? Well, based on the self-reported channels and capabilities, the Forrester report identifies five ‘flavours’ of service design that agencies mash up in different ways:

  1. ‘Fuzzy front-end’ innovation
  2. Physical and face-to-face experience design
  3. Digital experience design
  4. Organisational change partners
  5. Business process consulting

Most agencies practice two or three of these flavours, but there is a small group – including Designit – that matched four or all five of these patterns. These fifteen agencies mark a definitive move away from the previous pattern of specialised agencies and are leading the way into a truly holistic end-to-end experience, from front-end to back of house.

Even so, service design is far from a homogenous discipline, and agencies mix and match these five offerings in different ways. Here’s a quick look at some of the patterns we found most interesting here at Designit, from big shifts, to hints at trends to come:

  • The biggest group by far was digital experience design. These are firms that combine business strategy with specific design for web, mobile, and tablets. Nearly two thirds of the agencies that responded to the Forrester survey fall into this group – and we can only expect to see this number increase.
  • Meanwhile, as service migrates to digital, the ‘canonical’ branch of service design, i.e.: dealing with physical and face-to-face experiences, is much smaller. This year, fewer than a third of the respondents match this pattern.
  • Overall, business process consulting is the least-matched pattern, but Designit and the other full-service agencies hint at a new trend, with eleven of these fifteen agencies matching this pattern. Indeed, again unlike the other agencies, the report shows that firms in the group with Designit are just as likely to think in terms of business strategy as experience strategy.

Another interesting emerging micro-trend – and another interesting exception – is a move across the pond. Shifting from the European model, nine of the fifteen large, full-service agencies have their headquarters in North America, with eight in the US alone.

Our biggest takeaway? It’s all digital, baby.

To say digital’s been growing for years is, itself, pretty much the understatement of the year. But by now it’s really no surprise to see the vendor landscape and offerings reflect that – for sure, design for web, mobile, and tablet are all key offerings at Designit. Service design as a discipline is channel-agnostic, but as more of the customer’s experience becomes digital, it’s only natural that service design, too, becomes increasingly digital. And with more control over the experience, for both brand and user, digital can make it easier to provide better, user-centred service experiences.

And as service migrates to digital, demand for physical and face-to-face service design naturally falls. We won’t see the extinction of old school any time soon, though. Physical remains an essential part of the service experience in many industries, from retail to healthcare.

At Designit, newly partnered with Wipro Digital, we believe we are leading the way into 2016 with our own full-service offering.

The media's current obsession with celebrity - good or bad?

Current obsession? Celebrity worship has been around for millennia. Gladiators were the rock stars of the Roman world- the people of Pompeii didn’t need social media to obsess over celebrities, graffiti did the trick: ‘Celadus the Thracian gladiator is the delight of all the girls’. Gladiators were even known to give celebrity product endorsements.

So an obsession with celebrities is hardly a new thing, but then again it hardly goes to prove that it’s a good thing. Besides, a Youtube video can spread faster and wider than any graffito. This age of connectivity has encouraged more and more trivial 'news'- the kind of information only stalkers used to know (...not that I was a stalker). It’s as if the more we’re able to know, the more we want to know.

And this had opened a great big can of worms.

Many have an obsession with celebrity, but a few have an obsession with being a celebrity. This deluge of information has made them want it, and allowed them to get it. So now it’s a lose-lose situation. We’re invading the privacy of the well-balanced celebrities, and enabling the narcissism of the rest. Is this level of information healthy for either of us? It just feels so vacuous- couldn't our thirst for knowledge be better sated on current events? (Or Romans… whatever.) Is the media even going so far as to ‘distract’ us with celebrity gossip? I’ve heard accusations that the American media has been ignoring the Ukrainian riots in favour of the arrest of Justin Bieber. Call me paranoid, but discontent has been festering since the NSA and Snowden scandals… Well, the Roman senators always knew how to put on a big show, at any rate.
